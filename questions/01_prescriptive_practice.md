**Question 1** [Easy] - Knowledge
Which phase of clinical trials is the first to test an investigational drug in human subjects and primarily evaluates safety and pharmacokinetics?

A) Phase I
B) Phase II
C) Phase III
D) Phase IV

**Correct Answer: A**
**Rationale:** Phase I clinical trials are the first stage of testing in human subjects. They typically involve 20-80 healthy volunteers and are designed to assess the safety, tolerability, pharmacokinetics, and pharmacodynamics of the drug. Efficacy is not the primary objective at this stage. Phase II evaluates efficacy in patients, Phase III is large-scale randomized testing, and Phase IV is post-marketing surveillance.

A) Abbreviated New Drug Application (ANDA)

B) To conduct post-marketing surveillance and detect rare adverse effects
C) To compare the new drug to existing treatments in a randomized controlled trial
D) To establish the pharmacokinetic profile of the drug

**Correct Answer: B**

**Question 4** [Hard] - Case Study

C) Off-label prescribing is legal and common, but the prescriber assumes greater liability and should document rationale

**Correct Answer: C**

**Question 12** [Easy] - Knowledge

B) Neither the participants nor the researchers know who is receiving the investigational drug versus placebo
C) The study is conducted at two different sites
D) The drug is tested in two different dosage forms

**Correct Answer: B**

**Question 13** [Moderate] - Knowledge
Which of the following is TRUE regarding the FDA's accelerated approval pathway?

A) It eliminates the need for Phase III clinical trials
B) It allows approval based on a surrogate endpoint reasonably likely to predict clinical benefit, with confirmatory trials required post-approval
C) It is available only for generic drugs
D) It guarantees permanent market approval without further study

**Correct Answer: B**
**Rationale:** The FDA's accelerated approval pathway allows drugs for serious conditions that fill an unmet medical need to be approved based on a surrogate endpoint (such as tumor shrinkage or CD4 count improvement) that is reasonably likely to predict clinical benefit, rather than requiring demonstration of the clinical endpoint itself (such as improved survival). The sponsor is required to conduct confirmatory post-approval (Phase IV) trials to verify the actual clinical benefit. If confirmatory trials fail, the FDA can withdraw approval.

C) Phase I, Phase II, Phase III, Phase IV

B) Abbreviated New Drug Application (ANDA)
C) Biologics License Application (BLA) under the 351(k) pathway

**Correct Answer: C**

**Rationale:** This scenario illustrates the value of bar-code medication administration (BCMA) as a final safety checkpoint in the medication administration process. Despite earlier safety checks (pharmacist verification, nurse pulling medications), an error still occurred. The BCMA system caught the "wrong patient" error at the point of administration -- the last opportunity to prevent the error from reaching the patient. This aligns with the IOM's recommendation to use information technology as a safety net within the medication use system. **Question 93** [Moderate] - Case Study
An NP in an outpatient clinic receives an alert on her e-prescribing system for a potential drug-drug interaction every time she prescribes a statin with a calcium channel blocker, even when the interaction is not clinically significant. A) Clinical decision fatigue
B) Alert fatigue -- the tendency for providers to override or ignore alerts due to an overwhelming number of clinically insignificant warnings, which can lead to missing truly important alerts
C) Technology aversion
D) Prescriber burnout

**Correct Answer: B**
**Rationale:** Alert fatigue occurs when healthcare providers are exposed to an excessive number of alerts, many of which are clinically insignificant or irrelevant, leading them to override or ignore all alerts -- including those that are critically important. Studies show that providers override up to 90% of drug interaction alerts. This undermines the safety benefit of clinical decision support systems. Solutions include tiering alerts by severity, suppressing low-risk alerts, customizing alerts to the clinical context, and ensuring that high-severity alerts are visually distinct and require active acknowledgment. Alert fatigue is recognized as a major barrier to the effective use of health IT for medication safety.

**Question 98** [Moderate] - Case Study
An NP is writing a prescription for insulin for a 55-year-old patient with type 2 diabetes. She writes: "Insulin 10U SC qAM." According to safe prescribing practices and The Joint Commission guidelines, what error(s) does this prescription contain?

A) No errors; this prescription is appropriately written
B) "U" should be written out as "units" and "qAM" should be written as "every morning" to avoid misinterpretation; additionally, the specific insulin type (e.g., insulin glargine) is not specified
C) The route "SC" should be changed to "IM"
D) The dose of 10 units is too low for any patient

**Correct Answer: B**
**Rationale:** This prescription contains multiple errors: (1) "U" is on The Joint Commission's "Do Not Use" list and must be written out as "units" because "10U" can be misread as "100" (ten-fold overdose); (2) the abbreviation "qAM" could be misinterpreted -- writing "every morning" is clearer; (3) the specific type of insulin is not specified (insulin glargine, insulin lispro, insulin regular, etc.), which could lead to the wrong product being dispensed. A safe prescription would read: "Insulin glargine 10 units subcutaneously every morning." Precise, unambiguous prescription writing is a fundamental responsibility of every prescriber.

**Question 99** [Hard] - Case Study
A 68-year-old female is admitted to the hospital for pneumonia. During admission, the nurse performs medication reconciliation and identifies the following home medications: warfarin 5mg daily, metformin 1000mg BID, amlodipine 5mg daily, and omeprazole 20mg daily. Upon review, the NP discovers that glipizide 10mg BID was entered instead of metformin 1000mg BID (a CPOE selection error from the drop-down menu). Which combination of health IT interventions would BEST prevent this type of error?

A) Removing all electronic systems and returning to paper orders
B) Implementing tall-man lettering in the CPOE drug database, adding a weight-based clinical decision support alert for hypoglycemics, requiring verification of the medication reconciliation list before CPOE order entry, and training on careful drug selection from electronic menus
C) Relying solely on the pharmacy to catch all CPOE errors
D) Eliminating the drop-down menu and requiring free-text entry of all drug names

**Correct Answer: B**

**Question 100** [Moderate] - Knowledge
Which of the following statements BEST reflects the overarching objective of prescriptive practice as taught in pharmacology?

A) The primary goal is to prescribe the newest and most expensive medication available
B) The objective of drug therapy is to provide maximum benefit with minimum harm, using evidence-based practice to guide prescribing decisions while considering patient-specific factors including safety, efficacy, cost, and adherence
C) The goal is to prescribe as few medications as possible regardless of the patient's clinical needs
D) The primary objective is to ensure the patient never experiences any side effects

**Correct Answer: B**
**Rationale:** The overarching objective of prescriptive practice is to provide maximum benefit with minimum harm. This is achieved through evidence-based prescribing that considers drug effectiveness, safety, and selectivity; patient-specific factors such as age, organ function, comorbidities, and concurrent medications; economic considerations that affect adherence; patient education and shared decision-making; and ongoing monitoring for therapeutic response and adverse effects. There is no such thing as a perfectly safe or perfectly selective drug -- the prescriber's role is to optimize the risk-benefit ratio for each individual patient while applying the ethical principles of beneficence, non-maleficence, autonomy, and justice.
