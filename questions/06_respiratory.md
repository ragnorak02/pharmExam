**Question 1** [Easy] - Knowledge
Which statement best describes the primary mechanism of action of inhaled corticosteroids (ICS) in asthma management?

A) They directly relax bronchial smooth muscle to relieve acute bronchospasm
B) They suppress airway inflammation and promote synthesis of beta-2 adrenergic receptors
C) They block histamine receptors to reduce mucus production
D) They inhibit leukotriene synthesis exclusively at the alveolar level

**Correct Answer: B**
**Rationale:** Inhaled corticosteroids are the most effective anti-asthma drugs available. Their primary mechanism involves suppressing the inflammatory process in the airways and promoting the synthesis of beta-2 adrenergic receptors, which enhances responsiveness to beta-2 agonists. They do not directly relax bronchial smooth muscle and are not used for acute bronchospasm relief.

**Question 2** [Moderate] - Case Study
A 34-year-old female with persistent asthma has been using her fluticasone inhaler for 3 weeks but reports white patches on her tongue and sore throat. She demonstrates her inhaler technique by placing the MDI directly in her mouth and inhaling. What is the most appropriate intervention?

A) Discontinue the inhaled corticosteroid and switch to a leukotriene antagonist
B) Prescribe oral fluconazole and continue the current technique
C) Add a spacer device and instruct the patient to gargle and rinse after each use
D) Switch to an oral corticosteroid to avoid oropharyngeal side effects

**Correct Answer: C**
**Rationale:** The white patches indicate oropharyngeal candidiasis, a common local adverse effect of inhaled corticosteroids. Using a spacer device reduces oropharyngeal deposition of the drug, and gargling/rinsing the mouth after each use further minimizes the risk of candidiasis and dysphonia. Discontinuing the ICS is unnecessary, and switching to oral corticosteroids would increase systemic side effects significantly.

B) Fixed, regular schedule for long-term prophylaxis

**Question 19** [Moderate] - Case Study
A 42-year-old man using fluticasone MDI 220 mcg twice daily reports that his asthma symptoms are not well controlled. He uses his rescue inhaler about 6 times per week. When asked to demonstrate his inhaler technique, he exhales, places the MDI in his mouth, inhales rapidly, and immediately exhales. What is the most likely reason for poor control?

A) The fluticasone dose is too low
B) He needs an oral corticosteroid instead
C) His inhaler technique is incorrect -- he should inhale slowly and deeply, then hold his breath for 10 seconds
D) Fluticasone is the wrong ICS for his condition

**Correct Answer: C**
**Rationale:** Improper inhaler technique is a leading cause of poor asthma control. Before increasing the dose or changing medications, the NP should always assess and correct inhaler technique.

In acute heart disease, nicotine's cardiovascular stimulant effects (increased heart rate, vasoconstriction) could worsen the condition. NRT is appropriate for elderly patients and those with varying smoking histories.
