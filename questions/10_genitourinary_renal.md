**Question 1** [Easy] - Knowledge
Which class of medications is first-line pharmacotherapy for overactive bladder (OAB)?

A) Beta-3 adrenergic agonists
B) Antimuscarinic (anticholinergic) agents
C) Alpha-adrenergic blockers
D) 5-alpha reductase inhibitors

**Correct Answer: B**
**Rationale:** Antimuscarinic (anticholinergic) agents such as oxybutynin, tolterodine, solifenacin, and darifenacin are first-line pharmacotherapy for OAB. They work by blocking muscarinic M3 receptors on the detrusor muscle, reducing involuntary bladder contractions. However, behavioral therapy (bladder training, pelvic floor exercises, fluid management) should be initiated before or alongside pharmacotherapy.

**Question 3** [Easy] - Knowledge
What are the common side effects of oxybutynin (Ditropan)?

A) Diarrhea, hypotension, bradycardia
B) Dry mouth, dry eyes, constipation, blurred vision, dyspepsia, UTI secondary to urinary retention
C) Weight gain, edema, hyperkalemia
D) Headache, nausea, increased salivation

**Correct Answer: B**
**Rationale:** Oxybutynin is an antimuscarinic agent, and its side effects are predictable anticholinergic effects: dry mouth (most common), dry eyes, constipation, blurred vision, dyspepsia, cognitive impairment (especially in elderly), and urinary retention that can predispose to UTI. The extended-release formulation and transdermal patch have fewer anticholinergic side effects than the immediate-release oral form.

**Question 5** [Moderate] - Knowledge
How does mirabegron (Myrbetriq) differ from antimuscarinic agents in treating OAB?

A) Mirabegron is a beta-3 adrenergic agonist that relaxes the detrusor muscle without anticholinergic side effects
B) Mirabegron is a more potent anticholinergic than oxybutynin
C) Mirabegron works by increasing bladder capacity through alpha blockade
D) Mirabegron has no side effects

**Correct Answer: A**
**Rationale:** Mirabegron is a beta-3 adrenergic agonist that relaxes the detrusor smooth muscle during bladder filling, increasing bladder capacity. Unlike antimuscarinics, it does not cause dry mouth, constipation, or blurred vision. Its unique side effect profile includes potential blood pressure elevation, headache, constipation, cold symptoms (nasopharyngitis), and dizziness. Blood pressure should be monitored, and it is not recommended in patients with severe uncontrolled hypertension.

**Question 6** [Hard] - Case Study

A) Liver function tests monthly
B) Blood pressure monitoring, as mirabegron may increase BP; her baseline is already elevated
C) Serum potassium levels
D) Complete blood count weekly

**Correct Answer: B**
**Rationale:** Mirabegron can increase blood pressure by an average of 1-3 mmHg due to beta-3 receptor stimulation. **Question 9** [Hard] - Knowledge
Why should antimuscarinic agents be used with caution or avoided in elderly patients, particularly those with cognitive impairment?

A) Elderly patients are immune to anticholinergic side effects
B) Antimuscarinic agents cross the blood-brain barrier and can cause or worsen cognitive impairment, confusion, delirium, and increase dementia risk in elderly patients
C) Elderly patients metabolize anticholinergics too quickly
D) Antimuscarinic agents only work in patients under age 50

**Correct Answer: B**
**Rationale:** Anticholinergic medications can cross the blood-brain barrier and block central muscarinic receptors important for cognition and memory. Elderly patients are particularly vulnerable due to age-related changes in blood-brain barrier permeability, reduced hepatic metabolism, and lower baseline cholinergic reserve. The American Geriatrics Society Beers Criteria lists many anticholinergics as potentially inappropriate in older adults. If antimuscarinic therapy is needed, agents with lower CNS penetration (darifenacin, trospium) or mirabegron should be preferred.

**Question 13** [Moderate] - Knowledge
Which antimuscarinic agent has the highest selectivity for M3 receptors in the bladder and potentially fewer cognitive side effects?

A) Oxybutynin
B) Tolterodine
C) Darifenacin
D) Scopolamine

**Correct Answer: C**
**Rationale:** Darifenacin (Enablex) has the highest selectivity for M3 muscarinic receptors, which are the predominant subtype in the bladder detrusor muscle. This selectivity may result in fewer cognitive side effects (mediated by M1 receptors in the brain) compared to less selective agents like oxybutynin. However, M3 selectivity does not eliminate peripheral anticholinergic effects like dry mouth and constipation, as M3 receptors are also present in salivary glands and the GI tract.

**Question 15** [Moderate] - Case Study
A 58-year-old female with OAB and a history of uncontrolled narrow-angle glaucoma is referred for pharmacologic management. Which OAB medications are contraindicated?

A) Only oxybutynin is contraindicated
B) All antimuscarinic agents are contraindicated in uncontrolled narrow-angle glaucoma; mirabegron is a safer alternative
C) No OAB medications are contraindicated in glaucoma
D) Mirabegron is contraindicated in glaucoma

**Correct Answer: B**
**Rationale:** Antimuscarinic agents (oxybutynin, tolterodine, solifenacin, darifenacin, fesoterodine, trospium) are contraindicated in uncontrolled narrow-angle (angle-closure) glaucoma because pupillary dilation from muscarinic blockade can precipitate an acute glaucoma attack. Mirabegron (beta-3 agonist) has no anticholinergic properties and is a safer alternative. If antimuscarinics are considered, ophthalmology clearance is needed, and open-angle glaucoma is not a contraindication.

**Question 18** [Easy] - Case Study
A 46-year-old female with OAB asks about the oxybutynin transdermal patch (Oxytrol). What is the primary advantage of the transdermal formulation over oral oxybutynin?

A) The patch is more effective at treating OAB
B) The transdermal route bypasses first-pass hepatic metabolism, resulting in less dry mouth compared to oral formulations
C) The patch has no side effects
D) The patch works faster than oral forms

**Correct Answer: B**
**Rationale:** Oral oxybutynin undergoes extensive first-pass hepatic metabolism to N-desethyloxybutynin, an active metabolite primarily responsible for dry mouth. The transdermal patch bypasses hepatic first-pass metabolism, producing lower levels of this metabolite and significantly reducing the incidence of dry mouth (from ~60% oral to ~7-8% transdermal). The patch is applied to the abdomen, hip, or buttock twice weekly. Skin irritation at the application site is the most common patch-specific side effect.

A 52-year-old female floor nurse with OAB reports urgency and leakage episodes during her 12-hour shifts. She delays voiding due to patient care demands. What behavioral strategy is most important to recommend?

B) Implement timed voiding every 2-3 hours during shifts regardless of urge; performing Kegel exercises when urgency arises can help suppress the urge
C) Restrict all fluid intake during shifts

**Correct Answer: B**

**Question 20** [Hard] - Knowledge
A patient on solifenacin 10 mg daily for OAB develops urinary retention with a post-void residual of 250 mL. What is the mechanism of this adverse effect, and what is the appropriate action?

A) Solifenacin increases detrusor contractility excessively
B) Antimuscarinic blockade of M3 receptors on the detrusor muscle reduces contractile force, leading to incomplete emptying; reduce dose or discontinue and consider mirabegron
C) Urinary retention is unrelated to solifenacin
D) Increase the solifenacin dose to fully suppress bladder contractions

**Correct Answer: B**
**Rationale:** The therapeutic effect of antimuscarinics (suppressing involuntary detrusor contractions) can overshoot, reducing voluntary detrusor contractility enough to cause incomplete bladder emptying and retention. A post-void residual >150-200 mL is clinically significant. The solifenacin dose should be reduced or the drug discontinued. If OAB treatment is still needed, mirabegron (which relaxes the detrusor during filling but does not impair contraction during voiding to the same degree) is a reasonable alternative.

**Question 21** [Easy] - Knowledge
What is stress urinary incontinence?

A) Involuntary urine leakage associated with a sudden, compelling desire to void
B) Involuntary urine leakage associated with physical exertion, coughing, laughing, or sneezing
C) Inability to empty the bladder completely
D) Urine leakage from cognitive inability to reach the toilet

**Correct Answer: B**
**Rationale:** Stress urinary incontinence (SUI) is the involuntary leakage of urine during activities that increase intra-abdominal pressure -- coughing, laughing, sneezing, lifting, or exercise. It results from weakness of the pelvic floor muscles and/or dysfunction of the urethral sphincter, allowing urine to escape when abdominal pressure exceeds urethral closure pressure. It is distinct from urge incontinence (OAB), which involves leakage with a sudden urge to void.

C) Tamsulosin 0.4 mg daily

**Correct Answer: B**

**Question 29** [Easy] - Case Study
A 45-year-old female asks about surgical options for her stress urinary incontinence that has not responded adequately to conservative management. What is the most common surgical procedure for SUI?

A) Bladder augmentation
B) Midurethral sling procedure
C) Cystectomy
D) Suprapubic catheter placement

**Correct Answer: B**
**Rationale:** The midurethral sling procedure (using synthetic mesh tape placed under the mid-urethra) is the gold standard surgical treatment for SUI. Types include retropubic (TVT) and transobturator (TOT) approaches. Risks include mesh erosion, voiding dysfunction, and infection. Autologous fascial slings are an alternative for patients who prefer to avoid synthetic mesh.

C) Urgency-related urine leakage
D) Incontinence caused by environmental barriers to reaching the toilet
