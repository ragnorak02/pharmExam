**Question 1** [Easy] - Knowledge
What is the primary goal of antiretroviral therapy (ART) for HIV?

A) Eradicate HIV completely from the body
B) Suppress viral load to undetectable levels (<20-50 copies/mL), restore/preserve immune function, reduce morbidity/mortality, and prevent transmission
C) Increase the viral load to stimulate an immune response
D) Replace the immune system entirely with donor cells

**Correct Answer: B**
**Rationale:** The goals of ART are to suppress HIV viral load to undetectable levels (typically <20-50 copies/mL depending on the assay), restore and preserve CD4+ T-cell function, reduce HIV-related morbidity and mortality, and prevent HIV transmission to others. ART does not eradicate HIV -- the virus persists in latent reservoirs -- but sustained viral suppression allows immune reconstitution and essentially normal life expectancy.

**Question 2** [Moderate] - Knowledge
What is the recommended initial ART regimen for most treatment-naive patients with HIV?

A) A single NRTI taken once daily
B) Two NRTIs (backbone) plus an integrase strand transfer inhibitor (INSTI) as the preferred initial regimen
C) Three PIs taken together
D) An NNRTI monotherapy regimen

**Correct Answer: B**
**Rationale:** Current guidelines recommend two NRTIs (typically tenofovir/emtricitabine or abacavir/lamivudine) as the backbone combined with an INSTI (dolutegravir, bictegravir, or raltegravir) as the preferred initial regimen. INSTIs are preferred due to high efficacy, high barrier to resistance, favorable side effect profiles, and minimal drug interactions compared to PIs and NNRTIs. Common initial regimens include bictegravir/tenofovir AF/emtricitabine (Biktarvy) and dolutegravir/abacavir/lamivudine (Triumeq).

**Question 3** [Easy] - Knowledge
What does U=U mean in the context of HIV treatment?

A) Undertreated = Unresponsive
B) Undetectable = Untransmittable; a person with HIV who maintains an undetectable viral load on ART cannot sexually transmit HIV to partners
C) Unlimited = Uncurable
D) Undiagnosed = Untreatable

**Correct Answer: B**
**Rationale:** U=U (Undetectable = Untransmittable) is a scientifically validated concept supported by major studies (PARTNER, PARTNER2, Opposites Attract) demonstrating that people with HIV who achieve and maintain an undetectable viral load (<200 copies/mL) on ART have effectively zero risk of sexually transmitting HIV to their partners. This message reduces stigma, encourages treatment adherence, and promotes testing and treatment initiation.

**Question 4** [Hard] - Case Study
A 35-year-old male newly diagnosed with HIV has a CD4 count of 350 cells/mm3 and viral load of 85,000 copies/mL. He also takes omeprazole for GERD and simvastatin for hyperlipidemia. Resistance testing shows no mutations. Which initial ART regimen consideration is most important?

A) Delay ART until the CD4 count drops below 200
B) Start ART immediately regardless of CD4 count; assess for drug interactions, as some ART drugs (particularly PIs and some NNRTIs) interact with CYP3A4-metabolized drugs like simvastatin
C) Prescribe three NRTIs without any other class
D) Start with a PI-based regimen since it has fewer drug interactions

**Correct Answer: B**
**Rationale:** Current guidelines recommend starting ART immediately upon diagnosis regardless of CD4 count ("rapid ART" or "test and treat"). Drug interaction assessment is critical: PIs and the NNRTI efavirenz are potent CYP3A4 inhibitors/inducers and can significantly interact with simvastatin (risk of rhabdomyolysis with PI co-administration). An INSTI-based regimen (e.g., bictegravir/TAF/FTC) is preferred as INSTIs have fewer CYP450 interactions. Resistance testing should be completed before selecting the regimen.

**Question 5** [Moderate] - Case Study
A 42-year-old female with HIV on ART presents for routine follow-up. Her viral load has been undetectable for 2 years on bictegravir/tenofovir AF/emtricitabine (Biktarvy). She asks if she can stop taking her medication since she feels well. What is the correct response?

A) Yes, she can safely stop ART since her viral load is undetectable
B) No, ART must be continued lifelong; discontinuation will result in viral rebound from latent reservoirs, rising viral load, declining CD4 count, and risk of disease progression and transmission
C) She can take a drug holiday for 6 months and restart
D) She can switch to a once-monthly regimen and take medications only during that month

**Correct Answer: B**
**Rationale:** ART must be taken continuously and lifelong. HIV persists in latent reservoirs (resting CD4 cells, lymphoid tissue) despite undetectable plasma viral load. Discontinuing ART leads to viral rebound (usually within weeks), declining CD4 count, risk of opportunistic infections, and resumption of HIV transmissibility. The SMART trial demonstrated that treatment interruptions increased the risk of opportunistic infections and death. Adherence counseling and addressing barriers to lifelong therapy are essential.

**Question 6** [Moderate] - Knowledge
What level of adherence to ART is required for sustained viral suppression?

A) 50% adherence is sufficient
B) Greater than 95% adherence is critical for achieving and maintaining viral suppression and preventing resistance
C) 70% adherence is adequate for most regimens
D) Adherence does not affect treatment outcomes

**Correct Answer: B**
**Rationale:** Adherence >95% is traditionally considered necessary for optimal viral suppression (though newer INSTI-based regimens may be more forgiving). Missing even a few doses can allow viral replication at subtherapeutic drug levels, selecting for resistant mutations. Resistance can lead to treatment failure and limited future options. Adherence strategies include simplified regimens (single-tablet, once-daily), pillboxes, phone reminders, addressing side effects, treating depression/substance use, and regular follow-up.

**Question 7** [Easy] - Knowledge
Which ART drug class works by inhibiting the integrase enzyme, preventing HIV DNA from integrating into the host cell genome?

A) NRTIs (nucleoside reverse transcriptase inhibitors)
B) NNRTIs (non-nucleoside reverse transcriptase inhibitors)
C) INSTIs (integrase strand transfer inhibitors)
D) PIs (protease inhibitors)

**Correct Answer: C**
**Rationale:** Integrase strand transfer inhibitors (INSTIs) -- dolutegravir, bictegravir, raltegravir, elvitegravir, cabotegravir -- block the HIV integrase enzyme, which is responsible for inserting (integrating) viral DNA into the host cell chromosome. Without integration, the provirus cannot be established, and new viral particles cannot be produced. INSTIs are now the preferred third agent in initial ART regimens due to their high efficacy, high genetic barrier to resistance (dolutegravir, bictegravir), and favorable tolerability.

**Question 8** [Hard] - Case Study
A 50-year-old male with HIV and chronic hepatitis B (HBV) co-infection has been on tenofovir disoproxil fumarate/emtricitabine/dolutegravir. He wants to switch regimens due to concerns about tenofovir's renal effects. Why must any regimen change be carefully managed in this patient?

A) Changing ART has no implications for hepatitis B
B) Tenofovir and emtricitabine are active against both HIV and HBV; discontinuing them without an HBV-active alternative can cause a severe, potentially fatal HBV flare from immune reconstitution and viral rebound
C) Hepatitis B is cured by HIV medications
D) Only emtricitabine affects hepatitis B

**Correct Answer: B**
**Rationale:** Both tenofovir and emtricitabine have activity against HBV. In HIV/HBV co-infected patients, discontinuing these agents without substituting another HBV-active drug can trigger severe HBV reactivation with hepatic flare, potentially leading to hepatic decompensation and death. If a regimen change is needed, HBV-active agents must be maintained (e.g., switch to tenofovir AF instead of TDF, or add entecavir). HBV status should be assessed in all patients before starting or changing ART.

**Question 9** [Moderate] - Knowledge
Which monitoring parameters are essential for patients on ART?

A) CD4 count and viral load only at diagnosis
B) CD4 count, HIV viral load, renal function, hepatic function, lipid panel, and metabolic parameters at regular intervals
C) Only viral load annually
D) No monitoring is needed once ART is started

**Correct Answer: B**
**Rationale:** Regular monitoring on ART includes: HIV viral load (at baseline, 2-4 weeks after starting/changing ART, then every 3-6 months until suppressed, then every 6 months if stable), CD4 count (baseline, then every 3-6 months initially, can extend to annually if stable and >300), renal function (especially with tenofovir), hepatic function, fasting lipid panel and glucose (some ART classes cause dyslipidemia and insulin resistance), CBC, and screening for opportunistic infections. Drug resistance testing is done at baseline and upon treatment failure.

**Question 10** [Moderate] - Case Study
A 28-year-old male newly diagnosed with HIV has an HLA-B*5701 allele test result that is positive. Which NRTI must be avoided in this patient?

A) Tenofovir
B) Emtricitabine
C) Abacavir
D) Lamivudine

**Correct Answer: C**
**Rationale:** HLA-B*5701 positivity is a pharmacogenomic marker for abacavir hypersensitivity reaction (HSR), a potentially fatal multi-organ reaction involving fever, rash, GI symptoms, and respiratory symptoms. HLA-B*5701 screening is mandatory before prescribing abacavir. If positive, abacavir is absolutely contraindicated and should never be administered. The patient should be documented as abacavir-allergic. Alternative NRTI backbones (tenofovir-based) should be used.

**Question 11** [Easy] - Knowledge
Should all pregnant women with HIV receive antiretroviral therapy?

A) Only if their CD4 count is below 200
B) Yes, all pregnant women with HIV should receive ART regardless of CD4 count or viral load to prevent mother-to-child transmission
C) Only during the third trimester
D) ART is contraindicated in pregnancy

**Correct Answer: B**
**Rationale:** All pregnant women with HIV should receive ART regardless of CD4 count or viral load. The primary goals are to protect maternal health and prevent mother-to-child transmission (PMTCT) of HIV. ART should be initiated as early as possible in pregnancy (or continued if already on ART). The goal is to achieve an undetectable viral load (<50 copies/mL) by the time of delivery, which reduces vertical transmission to <1%.

**Question 12** [Moderate] - Case Study
A 30-year-old pregnant female with HIV at 34 weeks gestation has a viral load of 1,500 copies/mL despite ART adherence. What delivery-related interventions should be planned?

A) Vaginal delivery is preferred regardless of viral load
B) Scheduled cesarean section at 38 weeks is recommended since her viral load is >1,000 copies/mL, along with intrapartum IV zidovudine
C) No special delivery interventions are needed
D) Delay delivery until 42 weeks to allow more time for viral suppression

**Correct Answer: B**
**Rationale:** When the HIV viral load is >1,000 copies/mL at or near delivery (assessed at 36 weeks), a scheduled cesarean section at 38 weeks is recommended to reduce the risk of intrapartum HIV transmission to the infant. Intrapartum IV zidovudine should also be administered during labor/delivery when the viral load is >1,000 copies/mL. If the viral load is <1,000 copies/mL (especially if undetectable), vaginal delivery is generally safe. The regimen should be assessed for resistance and adherence should be addressed.

**Question 13** [Hard] - Case Study
A 26-year-old pregnant female newly diagnosed with HIV at 8 weeks gestation asks about which ART medications are safe during pregnancy. She is concerned about reports of birth defects with certain HIV medications. Which ART agent was previously associated with neural tube defect concerns in early pregnancy?

A) Tenofovir
B) Emtricitabine
C) Efavirenz
D) Raltegravir

**Correct Answer: C**
**Rationale:** Efavirenz (an NNRTI) was historically avoided in the first trimester of pregnancy due to concerns about neural tube defects (anencephaly, meningomyelocele) based on animal studies and initial case reports. While more recent data suggest the risk may be lower than initially feared, efavirenz is generally still not preferred for initiation in pregnancy, especially during the first trimester. Dolutegravir was also briefly cautioned after a signal from the Tsepamo study in Botswana, but subsequent data showed the neural tube defect risk was very small, and dolutegravir is now considered safe and is a preferred agent in pregnancy.

**Question 14** [Moderate] - Knowledge
What prophylaxis should be given to an infant born to an HIV-positive mother?

A) No prophylaxis is needed if the mother was on ART
B) Infant zidovudine (AZT) for 4-6 weeks starting within 6-12 hours of birth; additional agents may be added if maternal viral load was not suppressed
C) Adult-dose ART for 6 months
D) Only breastfeeding provides adequate prophylaxis

**Correct Answer: B**
**Rationale:** All infants born to HIV-positive mothers should receive zidovudine (AZT) prophylaxis starting within 6-12 hours of birth for 4-6 weeks. If the mother's viral load was >50 copies/mL near delivery, or she did not receive adequate ART, the infant may receive enhanced prophylaxis (zidovudine + lamivudine + nevirapine or raltegravir) for the first 2-6 weeks. The infant should be tested for HIV at birth, 14-21 days, 1-2 months, and 4-6 months using HIV DNA PCR (not antibody testing).

**Question 15** [Easy] - Knowledge
In resource-rich settings (like the United States), what is the recommendation regarding breastfeeding for HIV-positive mothers?

A) Breastfeeding is encouraged to transfer protective antibodies
B) Breastfeeding is generally NOT recommended because HIV can be transmitted through breast milk, even if the mother has an undetectable viral load; formula feeding is the safest option
C) Mixed feeding (breast milk and formula) is recommended
D) Breastfeeding is required for the first 6 months

**Correct Answer: B**
**Rationale:** In resource-rich settings (US, Europe), formula feeding is recommended for HIV-positive mothers because HIV can be transmitted through breast milk. While the risk is low if the mother is on suppressive ART (and evolving guidelines increasingly support shared decision-making for mothers on ART with sustained undetectable viral loads), complete avoidance of breastfeeding eliminates this transmission route entirely. In resource-limited settings where safe water and formula may not be available, the WHO recommends breastfeeding with ART, as the benefits outweigh the risks.

A 32-year-old female with HIV discovers she is pregnant at 10 weeks gestation. She has been on a stable regimen of bictegravir/tenofovir AF/emtricitabine (Biktarvy) with an undetectable viral load for 18 months. What is the appropriate action?

A) Discontinue all ART immediately due to teratogenicity concerns
B) Continue the current regimen if it is effective and well-tolerated; bictegravir/TAF/FTC is now considered a preferred regimen in pregnancy
C) Switch to efavirenz-based therapy for pregnancy
D) Stop ART and restart in the third trimester only

**Correct Answer: B**
**Rationale:** If a woman becomes pregnant while on a suppressive, well-tolerated ART regimen, it should generally be continued to maintain viral suppression and prevent transmission. Bictegravir/TAF/FTC (Biktarvy) is now included among preferred regimens in pregnancy based on accumulating safety data. Switching regimens risks viral rebound during the transition. The goal is to maintain undetectable viral load throughout pregnancy through delivery. Prenatal care should include close monitoring of viral load, CD4 count, and fetal development.

**Question 17** [Hard] - Knowledge
What are the key differences between intrapartum management for an HIV-positive mother with undetectable viral load versus one with a viral load >1,000 copies/mL?

A) There is no difference in management based on viral load
B) Undetectable viral load: vaginal delivery is appropriate, IV zidovudine is NOT routinely required; Viral load >1,000 at 36 weeks: scheduled cesarean at 38 weeks AND intrapartum IV zidovudine

**Question 20** [Hard] - Case Study
A 24-year-old female with HIV is planning pregnancy. She is currently on efavirenz/tenofovir DF/emtricitabine (Atripla) with an undetectable viral load. Her provider recommends switching to a different regimen before conception. What is the rationale?

A) Atripla is the preferred regimen for pregnancy
B) Efavirenz has historical teratogenicity concerns (neural tube defects), and tenofovir DF has more renal/bone toxicity than tenofovir AF; switching to a preferred pregnancy regimen (e.g., dolutegravir/TAF/FTC) before conception optimizes safety
C) There is no reason to change regimens
D) She should stop all ART before conception and restart in the second trimester

**Correct Answer: B**
**Rationale:** Pre-conception planning for HIV-positive women includes optimizing the ART regimen for pregnancy safety. Efavirenz carries historical neural tube defect concerns (particularly during the periconception and first trimester period), and tenofovir DF has greater renal and bone mineral density effects than tenofovir AF. Switching to a preferred pregnancy regimen -- such as dolutegravir/TAF/FTC (Biktarvy) or dolutegravir + TAF/FTC -- before conception ensures the safest regimen is in place during the critical organogenesis period. ART should never be discontinued.

**Question 21** [Easy] - Knowledge
What is HIV pre-exposure prophylaxis (PrEP)?

A) Treatment given after HIV exposure in an emergency
B) Daily or long-acting medication taken by HIV-negative individuals at substantial risk of HIV infection to prevent acquisition of the virus
C) A vaccine against HIV
D) Post-exposure treatment for healthcare workers after needlestick injuries

**Correct Answer: B**
**Rationale:** PrEP (pre-exposure prophylaxis) involves HIV-negative individuals taking antiretroviral medication before potential HIV exposure to prevent infection. When taken consistently, PrEP reduces the risk of HIV acquisition by approximately 99% for sexual transmission and >74% for injection drug use. It is a critical component of HIV prevention strategies alongside condom use, regular testing, and treatment of HIV-positive partners (U=U).

**Question 22** [Moderate] - Case Study
A 17-year-old male presents to the clinic requesting PrEP. He weighs 60 kg and reports having multiple male sexual partners. He is sexually active and does not consistently use condoms. What must be verified before initiating PrEP?

A) Only his age needs to be verified
B) Confirmed HIV-negative status (4th generation antigen/antibody test), renal function (creatinine/eGFR), hepatitis B status, STI screening, and assessment of risk factors; PrEP is FDA-approved for adolescents weighing >35 kg

D) Only a urine drug screen is needed

**Correct Answer: B**

**Question 23** [Moderate] - Knowledge
What are the two oral PrEP formulations currently available, and how do they differ?

A) Both contain the same medications with no differences
B) Truvada (tenofovir DF/emtricitabine) -- approved for all at-risk populations including receptive vaginal sex and IVDU; Descovy (tenofovir AF/emtricitabine) -- approved only for at-risk populations excluding receptive vaginal sex (insufficient data for cisgender women)
C) Truvada is for adults only; Descovy is for children only
D) They are different brands of the same drug with identical indications

**Correct Answer: B**

**Question 25** [Easy] - Knowledge
What is the daily dosing regimen for oral PrEP?

A) One pill taken three times daily
B) One pill (TDF/FTC or TAF/FTC) taken once daily, at approximately the same time each day
C) Two pills taken twice daily
D) One pill taken once weekly

**Correct Answer: B**

**Question 26** [Moderate] - Case Study

**Question 27** [Hard] - Knowledge
What is long-acting injectable cabotegravir (Apretude) for PrEP, and what are its advantages and considerations?

A) It is an oral medication taken weekly
B) It is an intramuscular injection given every 2 months (after 2 monthly initiation injections) that provides sustained HIV protection; advantages include elimination of daily pill burden and improved adherence; considerations include injection site reactions, need for clinic visits, and a "tail" of subtherapeutic drug levels after discontinuation
C) It is a topical gel applied daily
D) It is an annual subcutaneous implant

**Correct Answer: B**

**Question 28** [Moderate] - Case Study
A 19-year-old college student on PrEP tests positive for hepatitis B surface antigen (HBsAg) at his routine screening visit. He is currently taking Truvada (TDF/FTC). What is the critical consideration if he ever wants to stop PrEP?

A) Hepatitis B has no relationship to PrEP medications
B) TDF and FTC are both active against HBV; abruptly stopping PrEP can cause a severe HBV flare due to loss of HBV suppression and immune-mediated hepatic inflammation; HBV must be monitored closely and managed if PrEP is discontinued
C) He should stop PrEP immediately now that HBV is diagnosed
D) HBV infection means PrEP is no longer effective

**Correct Answer: B**
**Rationale:** Tenofovir DF and emtricitabine (components of Truvada) have antiviral activity against both HIV and HBV. In a PrEP patient with HBV co-infection, these drugs are also treating the hepatitis B. If PrEP is discontinued, the sudden removal of HBV suppression can trigger a severe hepatic flare with potentially serious consequences (acute liver failure). This is why HBV screening (HBsAg, anti-HBs, anti-HBc) is mandatory before starting PrEP. If PrEP is stopped, HBV should be monitored with liver function tests for several months, and alternative HBV treatment may be needed.

**Question 29** [Easy] - Case Study
A 22-year-old male presents to the clinic interested in PrEP. He is in a serodiscordant relationship (his male partner is HIV-positive). He has a 4th-generation HIV antigen/antibody test that returns positive. What is the immediate next step?

A) Start PrEP immediately
B) Do NOT start PrEP; a positive HIV test means he may already be infected; confirmatory testing (HIV-1/HIV-2 differentiation assay and viral load) is needed, and he should be referred for ART evaluation if confirmed positive
C) Start PrEP at double the usual dose
D) Retest in 6 months before making any decisions

**Correct Answer: B**
**Rationale:** PrEP must NEVER be prescribed to someone who is HIV-positive. Using only two antiretroviral agents (as in PrEP) in an HIV-positive individual provides inadequate treatment and rapidly selects for drug-resistant HIV, compromising future treatment options. A positive screening test requires confirmatory testing (HIV-1/HIV-2 antibody differentiation assay, and if needed, HIV RNA viral load). If confirmed HIV-positive, he should be referred for full ART initiation with a 3-drug regimen, not PrEP.

What is the recommended follow-up schedule and monitoring for patients on PrEP?

A) Annual visits only
B) Follow-up every 3 months with HIV testing (must remain negative), STI screening, renal function monitoring, pregnancy testing (if applicable), adherence assessment, risk reduction counseling, and hepatitis B monitoring if co-infected
C) No follow-up is needed once PrEP is started

**Correct Answer: B**

This structured follow-up ensures safe and effective PrEP use.
