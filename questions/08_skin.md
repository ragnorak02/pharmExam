**Question 1** [Easy] - Knowledge
Tinea pedis (athlete's foot) is caused by which type of organism?

A) Bacteria
B) Virus
C) Dermatophyte fungi
D) Parasites

**Correct Answer: C**
**Rationale:** Tinea pedis is caused by dermatophyte fungi, most commonly Trichophyton rubrum, T. mentagrophytes, or Epidermophyton floccosum. These organisms thrive in warm, moist environments such as shoes, locker rooms, and swimming pools. They infect keratinized tissue (skin, hair, nails) and produce enzymes that digest keratin.

**Question 2** [Easy] - Knowledge
Which topical antifungal agents are considered first-line for tinea pedis?

A) Topical corticosteroids
B) Topical mupirocin
C) Clotrimazole, miconazole, or terbinafine cream
D) Topical metronidazole

**Correct Answer: C**
**Rationale:** First-line treatment for tinea pedis is topical antifungal agents: azoles (clotrimazole, miconazole) or allylamines (terbinafine cream). All are available over the counter. Terbinafine may have a slightly higher cure rate and shorter treatment duration. Topical corticosteroids should not be used alone as they suppress the immune response and can worsen fungal infection.

**Question 7** [Moderate] - Knowledge
Which OTC topical antifungal agent works by inhibiting squalene epoxidase in the fungal cell membrane synthesis pathway?

A) Clotrimazole
B) Miconazole
C) Terbinafine
D) Nystatin

**Correct Answer: C**
**Rationale:** Terbinafine is an allylamine antifungal that works by inhibiting squalene epoxidase, an enzyme essential for ergosterol synthesis in the fungal cell membrane. This is a different mechanism than the azoles (clotrimazole, miconazole), which inhibit lanosterol 14-alpha-demethylase (CYP450-dependent enzyme), also in the ergosterol synthesis pathway. Both result in disrupted fungal cell membrane integrity.

**Question 16** [Easy] - Knowledge
KOH preparation is used to diagnose fungal infections by:

A) Culturing the organism on growth media
B) Dissolving epithelial cells with potassium hydroxide to reveal fungal hyphae or spores under microscopy
C) Measuring serum fungal antigen levels
D) Staining tissue with hematoxylin and eosin

**Correct Answer: B**
**Rationale:** KOH (potassium hydroxide) preparation is a rapid, inexpensive point-of-care diagnostic test. Skin scrapings, hair, or nail clippings are placed on a slide with 10-20% KOH solution, which dissolves keratin and epithelial cells while leaving fungal elements (branching hyphae, spores, pseudohyphae) intact for visualization under microscopy. It is the standard initial diagnostic test for suspected dermatophyte and Candida infections.

**Question 21** [Easy] - Knowledge
Topical corticosteroids are classified into how many potency groups?

A) Two (low and high)
B) Four (low, medium, high, super-high potency)
C) Seven (classes I-VII)
D) Three (mild, moderate, severe)

**Correct Answer: B**
**Rationale:** Topical corticosteroids are broadly classified into four potency groups: low potency (e.g., hydrocortisone 1%), medium potency (e.g., triamcinolone 0.1%), high potency (e.g., fluocinonide 0.05%), and super-high potency (e.g., clobetasol 0.05%). Some classification systems use seven classes (I being most potent, VII being least). The appropriate potency is selected based on the condition, body site, and patient age.

Topical corticosteroids are primarily used for which therapeutic purpose?

A) Treating fungal infections
B) Reducing inflammation and itching in dermatologic conditions
C) Promoting wound healing
D) Killing bacteria

**Correct Answer: B**

**Question 23** [Moderate] - Knowledge
Which adverse effect is most concerning with prolonged use of topical corticosteroids?

A) Hyperpigmentation
B) Atrophy of the dermis and epidermis (skin thinning)
C) Increased hair growth
D) Acne improvement

**Correct Answer: B**
**Rationale:** Prolonged use of topical corticosteroids causes atrophy of both the dermis and epidermis, leading to thin, fragile skin with visible blood vessels (telangiectasia), striae (stretch marks), easy bruising, and poor wound healing. Higher potency agents, occlusive dressings, and application to thin-skinned areas (face, groin, axillae) accelerate these changes. This is why the lowest effective potency should be used for the shortest duration necessary.

**Question 24** [Hard] - Case Study
A 3-year-old with moderate atopic dermatitis has been applying betamethasone dipropionate (high-potency) cream to the face and body for 8 weeks. The mother reports the child's growth has slowed at recent well-child visits. What is the most likely cause?

A) Nutritional deficiency
B) Systemic absorption of the high-potency topical corticosteroid causing adrenal suppression and growth retardation
C) Natural growth deceleration
D) Celiac disease

**Correct Answer: B**
**Rationale:** Children are particularly susceptible to systemic absorption of topical corticosteroids due to their higher body surface area-to-weight ratio and thinner skin. Prolonged use of high-potency agents, especially over large areas or thin skin (face), can suppress the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis, leading to adrenal suppression, growth retardation, and Cushingoid features. A high-potency agent like betamethasone dipropionate should never be used on the face or for extended periods in children. The FNP should taper the corticosteroid and switch to a low-potency agent or calcineurin inhibitor.

**Question 25** [Moderate] - Knowledge
Which body areas should AVOID application of high-potency topical corticosteroids?

A) Palms and soles
B) Face, groin, axillae, and intertriginous areas
C) Trunk and extremities
D) Scalp

**Correct Answer: B**
**Rationale:** High-potency topical corticosteroids should be avoided on the face, groin, axillae, and intertriginous (skin fold) areas because these sites have thin skin with increased absorption, making them more susceptible to steroid-induced atrophy, striae, and telangiectasia. Only low-potency agents (hydrocortisone 1-2.5%) should be used in these areas. Conversely, thick-skinned areas (palms, soles, elbows, knees) may require higher potency for adequate penetration.

A 7-year-old with atopic dermatitis has been well-controlled on hydrocortisone 1% cream but now has a flare on the antecubital fossae and neck. The parent asks about using the "stronger cream" (clobetasol 0.05%) that was prescribed for the father's psoriasis. What is the appropriate counseling?

**Question 27** [Easy] - Knowledge
How do occlusive dressings affect topical corticosteroid absorption?

A) They have no effect
B) They significantly increase absorption (up to 10-fold), increasing both efficacy and risk of adverse effects
C) They decrease absorption
D) They only affect antibiotic absorption

**Correct Answer: B**
**Rationale:** Occlusive dressings (plastic wrap, bandages, diapers in infants) increase hydration of the stratum corneum and significantly enhance percutaneous absorption of topical corticosteroids, potentially up to 10-fold. **Question 29** [Hard] - Knowledge
Calcineurin inhibitors (tacrolimus ointment and pimecrolimus cream) are used as alternatives to topical corticosteroids for atopic dermatitis. What is their primary advantage?

A) They are more potent anti-inflammatory agents than super-high-potency corticosteroids
B) They do not cause skin atrophy, making them suitable for long-term use on the face, eyelids, and intertriginous areas
C) They are less expensive than corticosteroids
D) They have no side effects

**Correct Answer: B**
**Rationale:** Topical calcineurin inhibitors (tacrolimus 0.03% and 0.1%, pimecrolimus 1%) work by inhibiting T-cell activation and cytokine release without the atrophogenic effects of corticosteroids. They are FDA-approved as second-line agents for atopic dermatitis. Common side effects include application-site burning/stinging, which typically diminishes with continued use. They carry an FDA black box warning regarding theoretical malignancy risk, though causation has not been established.

**Question 34** [Easy] - Knowledge
Which symptom is the hallmark of atopic dermatitis?

A) Pain
B) Intense pruritus (itching)
C) Purulent discharge
D) Vesicular rash

**Correct Answer: B**

Why is isotretinoin classified as FDA Pregnancy Category X, and what risk management program governs its prescribing?
