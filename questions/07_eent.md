**Question 1** [Easy] - Knowledge
Which type of glaucoma is characterized by a gradual, painless loss of peripheral vision due to progressive optic nerve damage?

A) Angle-closure glaucoma
B) Open-angle glaucoma
C) Normal-tension glaucoma
D) Congenital glaucoma

**Correct Answer: B**
**Rationale:** Open-angle glaucoma is the most common form and involves gradual, painless loss of peripheral vision. The trabecular meshwork becomes less efficient at draining aqueous humor over time, leading to increased IOP and optic nerve damage. Angle-closure glaucoma, by contrast, presents acutely with pain, redness, and sudden vision changes.

**Question 2** [Moderate] - Knowledge
The primary mechanism by which glaucoma medications reduce intraocular pressure (IOP) involves which two physiologic pathways?

A) Increasing aqueous humor production and constricting the pupil
B) Facilitating aqueous humor outflow and reducing aqueous humor production
C) Dilating the pupil and reducing vitreous humor volume
D) Increasing tear production and decreasing corneal edema

**Correct Answer: B**
**Rationale:** All glaucoma drugs work by either facilitating the outflow of aqueous humor from the anterior chamber or reducing its production by the ciliary body. Different drug classes target one or both of these mechanisms. Pupil constriction (miotics) is a means to facilitate outflow, not a separate pathway.

**Question 3** [Easy] - Knowledge
Which drug class is considered first-line topical therapy for open-angle glaucoma?

A) Miotics (pilocarpine)
B) Alpha-2 agonists (brimonidine)
C) Prostaglandin analogs (latanoprost)
D) Carbonic anhydrase inhibitors (dorzolamide)

**Correct Answer: C**
**Rationale:** Prostaglandin analogs such as latanoprost are considered first-line agents for open-angle glaucoma. They effectively lower IOP by facilitating uveoscleral outflow of aqueous humor, are dosed once daily (improving adherence), and have a favorable side effect profile compared to beta blockers. Beta blockers like timolol are also commonly used first-line agents.

**Question 4** [Moderate] - Case Study
A 58-year-old patient with open-angle glaucoma and a history of asthma presents for a medication review. The ophthalmologist started timolol 0.5% drops. The patient reports increased wheezing over the past two weeks. What is the most likely cause of the worsening respiratory symptoms?

A) Seasonal allergic rhinitis exacerbation
B) Systemic absorption of the topical beta blocker causing bronchospasm
C) An unrelated viral upper respiratory infection
D) Paradoxical bronchoconstriction from the preservative in the eye drops

**Correct Answer: B**
**Rationale:** Timolol is a nonselective beta blocker. Even when applied topically to the eye, it can be absorbed systemically through the nasolacrimal duct and conjunctival vasculature. In patients with asthma or COPD, systemic beta-2 blockade can precipitate bronchospasm. Timolol should be avoided or used with extreme caution in patients with reactive airway disease. The FNP should recommend switching to a non-beta-blocker agent such as latanoprost or brimonidine.

A) Latanoprost
B) Brimonidine
C) Timolol

**Question 7** [Moderate] - Knowledge

**Question 8** [Moderate] - Case Study

B) Timolol 0.5% drops once daily

**Question 9** [Easy] - Knowledge
Dorzolamide belongs to which drug class, and how does it reduce IOP?

A) Prostaglandin analog; increases uveoscleral outflow
B) Carbonic anhydrase inhibitor; reduces aqueous humor production
C) Miotic agent; constricts the pupil
D) Alpha agonist; increases trabecular outflow

**Correct Answer: B**
**Rationale:** Dorzolamide is a topical carbonic anhydrase inhibitor (CAI). It reduces IOP by inhibiting carbonic anhydrase in the ciliary body, which decreases the production of aqueous humor. It is used as adjunctive therapy or as an alternative for patients who cannot tolerate beta blockers.

**Question 10** [Moderate] - Knowledge
Pilocarpine reduces IOP by which mechanism?

A) Decreasing aqueous humor production by the ciliary body
B) Constricting the pupil (miosis), which opens the trabecular meshwork and increases outflow
C) Blocking muscarinic receptors to relax the ciliary muscle
D) Stimulating alpha-2 receptors to reduce aqueous production

**Correct Answer: B**
**Rationale:** Pilocarpine is a direct-acting cholinergic (muscarinic) agonist that causes miosis (pupil constriction). This pulls the iris root away from the trabecular meshwork, opening drainage channels and increasing aqueous humor outflow. It is particularly useful in angle-closure glaucoma. Side effects include blurred near vision and brow ache from ciliary muscle spasm.

**Question 12** [Hard] - Case Study
Continuing with DB (66-year-old, IOP 44 mmHg, acute angle-closure glaucoma): Which initial combination of topical medications is most appropriate while awaiting ophthalmology consultation?

B) Timolol + dorzolamide + brimonidine + pilocarpine
C) Atropine + phenylephrine + dexamethasone

**Question 13** [Easy] - Knowledge
Atropine eye drops cause which effects on the eye?

A) Miosis and accommodation
B) Cycloplegia (paralysis of accommodation) and mydriasis (pupil dilation)
C) Reduced IOP and miosis
D) Increased tear production and reduced photophobia

**Correct Answer: B**
**Rationale:** Atropine is an anticholinergic (muscarinic antagonist) that blocks parasympathetic input to the eye. This causes mydriasis (pupil dilation) by relaxing the iris sphincter and cycloplegia (paralysis of accommodation) by relaxing the ciliary muscle. Patients experience photophobia from the dilated pupil and difficulty with near vision from loss of accommodation.

---

**Question 14** [Moderate] - Knowledge
Which agent causes mydriasis WITHOUT cycloplegia?

A) Atropine
B) Cyclopentolate
C) Phenylephrine
D) Tropicamide

**Correct Answer: C**
**Rationale:** Phenylephrine is a direct-acting alpha-1 adrenergic agonist that dilates the pupil (mydriasis) by contracting the iris dilator muscle. Because it acts on sympathetic receptors rather than blocking parasympathetic (muscarinic) receptors, it does not cause cycloplegia. Atropine, cyclopentolate, and tropicamide are all anticholinergic agents that cause both mydriasis AND cycloplegia.

**Question 31** [Easy] - Knowledge

B) Corneal epithelial defects and abrasions

**Rationale:** In adults with penicillin allergy and acute bacterial sinusitis, doxycycline is a first-line alternative providing good coverage against typical sinusitis pathogens. Respiratory fluoroquinolones (levofloxacin, moxifloxacin) are also effective alternatives but should be reserved for patients who have failed other therapies due to concerns about fluoroquinolone-associated adverse effects (tendinopathy, QT prolongation, CNS effects).

**Question 77** [Hard] - Case Study
A 7-year-old with a confirmed penicillin allergy (anaphylaxis at age 3) presents with acute bacterial sinusitis (symptoms for 14 days without improvement). Which antibiotic is most appropriate?

A) Amoxicillin-clavulanate
B) Cefdinir or cefuroxime (with caution, as cross-reactivity with severe penicillin allergy is debated)
C) Doxycycline
D) Ciprofloxacin

**Correct Answer: B**
**Rationale:** In children with penicillin allergy, cefdinir or cefuroxime axetil are recommended alternatives for sinusitis. While there is historical concern about cephalosporin cross-reactivity with penicillin allergy, second- and third-generation cephalosporins have very low cross-reactivity rates (1-2%). However, with a history of anaphylaxis, shared clinical decision-making is important, and some clinicians may prefer to pursue penicillin allergy testing first. Doxycycline is generally avoided in children under 8 due to dental staining risk. Ciprofloxacin is not approved for sinusitis in children.

**Question 78** [Easy] - Knowledge
Intranasal corticosteroids (e.g., fluticasone) are used as adjunctive therapy in sinusitis to:

A) Kill bacteria directly
B) Reduce nasal mucosal inflammation and edema, facilitating sinus drainage
C) Replace oral antibiotics
D) Prevent viral transmission

**Correct Answer: B**
**Rationale:** Intranasal corticosteroids reduce inflammation and edema of the nasal mucosa and sinus ostia, promoting natural sinus drainage. They are used as adjunctive therapy alongside antibiotics in bacterial sinusitis and as monotherapy for allergic rhinitis. They have a slow onset (days to weeks for full effect) and minimal systemic absorption, making them safe for long-term use.

**Question 79** [Hard] - Case Study
A 35-year-old presents with facial pressure, purulent nasal discharge, and tooth pain for 3 weeks. He was treated with amoxicillin for 10 days with partial improvement but symptoms recurred after completing the course. CT sinus shows opacification of the right maxillary sinus. What is the next step?

A) Repeat the same course of amoxicillin
B) Escalate to amoxicillin-clavulanate or a respiratory fluoroquinolone; consider ENT referral if this second-line therapy fails
C) Observation for another 2 weeks
D) Intranasal corticosteroid alone

**Correct Answer: B**
**Rationale:** Treatment failure or early recurrence after first-line amoxicillin suggests a resistant organism or anatomic factor preventing drainage. Second-line therapy with amoxicillin-clavulanate or a respiratory fluoroquinolone (levofloxacin, moxifloxacin) is appropriate. If the patient fails second-line therapy, ENT referral for possible endoscopic evaluation, culture, and surgical intervention is warranted. This clinical picture (symptoms > 12 weeks) may represent the transition from acute to chronic sinusitis.

**Question 80** [Moderate] - Case Study
RH (49-year-old with HTN) returns 12 days later with worsening symptoms: now has facial pressure, purulent rhinorrhea, and fever of 101.5 degrees F. He has been using saline irrigation and cetirizine without improvement. What is the most appropriate treatment?

A) Continue symptomatic management for another week
B) Amoxicillin-clavulanate for 10 days, plus intranasal corticosteroid spray as adjunct
C) Pseudoephedrine with no other treatment
D) CT sinus and ENT referral as first step

**Correct Answer: B**
**Rationale:** RH now meets criteria for acute bacterial sinusitis: symptoms persisting beyond 10 days without improvement, with worsening symptoms (double-sickening pattern with fever). Amoxicillin-clavulanate is appropriate as first-line therapy (given his worsening course, broader coverage is reasonable). Intranasal corticosteroid spray helps reduce mucosal inflammation and promote drainage. CT imaging is not needed for the initial diagnosis of uncomplicated ABS and is reserved for recurrent or chronic cases or suspected complications.
