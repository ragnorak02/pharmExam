**Question 1** [Easy] - Knowledge
Which of the following best defines drug absorption?

A) The enzymatic alteration of a drug's chemical structure
B) The movement of a drug from its site of administration into the blood
C) The movement of a drug throughout the body after entering the bloodstream
D) The removal of a drug from the body via the kidneys

**Correct Answer: B**
**Rationale:** Absorption is specifically defined as the movement of a drug from its site of administration into the bloodstream. Option A describes metabolism (biotransformation), option C describes distribution, and option D describes excretion.

**Question 2** [Easy] - Knowledge
Which factor does NOT directly affect the rate of drug absorption from the GI tract?

A) Dissolution rate of the drug formulation
B) Surface area available for absorption
C) Plasma protein binding capacity
D) Blood flow to the site of absorption

**Correct Answer: C**
**Rationale:** Plasma protein binding affects distribution, not absorption. Dissolution rate, surface area, and blood flow at the absorption site are all primary factors that influence how quickly a drug moves from its administration site into the bloodstream.

**Question 13** [Moderate] - Knowledge
The first-pass effect refers to:

B) Rapid hepatic metabolism of an orally administered drug before it reaches systemic circulation
C) The first dose of a loading dose regimen
D) Initial binding of a drug to plasma proteins after IV administration

**Question 17** [Easy] - Knowledge
Drugs cross cell membranes most readily when they are:

A) Ionized and water-soluble
B) Lipid-soluble and non-ionized
C) Bound to plasma proteins
D) Large molecular weight compounds

**Correct Answer: B**

**Question 18** [Hard] - Knowledge
Aspirin (acetylsalicylic acid, pKa 3.5) is a weak acid. In which environment will aspirin be predominantly in its non-ionized, absorbable form?

A) The stomach (pH 1-2)
B) The duodenum (pH 6)
C) The ileum (pH 7.4)
D) The colon (pH 7-8)

**Correct Answer: A**
**Rationale:** Weak acids are predominantly non-ionized in acidic environments (low pH) and ionized in basic environments (high pH). Since aspirin is a weak acid with a pKa of 3.5, it will be mostly non-ionized in the highly acidic stomach (pH 1-2), where the pH is well below its pKa. This is the principle that acidic drugs ionize in basic media and vice versa. The non-ionized form is lipid-soluble and crosses membranes readily.

**Question 25** [Hard] - Case Study
A nurse practitioner is managing a patient taking a medication that is a weak base (pKa 8.0). The patient develops renal tubular acidosis, causing urine pH to drop from 6.0 to 4.5. How will this change affect renal excretion of this drug?

A) Excretion will increase because the drug becomes more ionized in acidic urine and cannot be reabsorbed
B) Excretion will decrease because acidic urine promotes passive reabsorption
C) Excretion will remain unchanged because urine pH does not affect drug elimination
D) Excretion will increase because the drug becomes more lipid-soluble

**Correct Answer: A**
**Rationale:** Weak bases become more ionized (protonated) in acidic environments. In more acidic urine (pH 4.5), this weak base will be predominantly ionized. Ionized drugs are polar/water-soluble and cannot passively diffuse back across the renal tubular membrane for reabsorption. Therefore, more drug remains trapped in the urine and is excreted. This is the ion-trapping principle: acidic drugs ionize in basic media and basic drugs ionize in acidic media.

**Question 30** [Easy] - Knowledge
When a drug is bound to plasma albumin, it:

A) Can freely exit the vascular system to reach target tissues
B) Cannot leave the vascular system, is pharmacologically inactive, and is not metabolized or excreted
C) Is immediately metabolized by hepatic enzymes
D) Is rapidly excreted by the kidneys through glomerular filtration

**Correct Answer: B**

Bound drug is pharmacologically inactive because it cannot reach receptors, and it is not available for metabolism or renal excretion. Only the free (unbound) fraction of a drug is pharmacologically active and subject to distribution, metabolism, and excretion.

**Question 32** [Hard] - Case Study
A patient taking warfarin (99% protein-bound) is started on a sulfonamide antibiotic that also has high affinity for albumin binding sites. Two days later, the patient's INR rises from a therapeutic 2.5 to a dangerously elevated 5.8. What mechanism best explains this interaction?

A) The sulfonamide inhibited CYP450 enzymes that metabolize warfarin
B) The sulfonamide displaced warfarin from albumin binding sites, increasing free warfarin levels
C) The sulfonamide increased warfarin absorption from the GI tract
D) The sulfonamide decreased renal excretion of warfarin

**Correct Answer: B**
**Rationale:** This is a classic protein binding displacement interaction. Warfarin is 99% bound to albumin, meaning only 1% is free and active. When the sulfonamide competes for the same albumin binding sites, it displaces warfarin, dramatically increasing the free (active) fraction. Even a small displacement (e.g., from 99% bound to 97% bound) triples the free drug concentration (from 1% to 3%), significantly increasing the anticoagulant effect and bleeding risk.

**Question 84** [Hard] - Case Study

A) Loading doses are cheaper than maintenance doses
B) Without a loading dose, it would take approximately 12 days (4 x 72 hours) to reach steady state, which is too long to wait for therapeutic benefit
C) Loading doses prevent first-pass metabolism
D) Loading doses bypass the need for drug distribution

**Correct Answer: B**

A 52-year-old patient takes the following medications daily: lisinopril 20 mg (renally excreted), simvastatin 40 mg (CYP3A4 substrate), metformin 1000 mg BID (renally excreted), and omeprazole 20 mg (CYP2C19 substrate and inhibitor). **Correct Answer: C**
**Rationale:** This is a complex scenario involving opposing effects on the same drug. Grapefruit juice inhibits intestinal CYP3A4, which would increase simvastatin bioavailability and potentially raise levels to toxic concentrations (risking rhabdomyolysis).
